STORY ONE
Japan Launches Sovereign Physical AI Consortium — SoftBank, NEC, Sony, Honda Bet ¥1 Trillion on Domestic Foundation Model
Nikkei Asia · TechWire Asia · Japan Times · April 12–14, 2026

On April 12, four of Japan's largest industrial groups — ソフトバンク (SoftBank), 日本電気 (NEC), ソニーグループ (Sony Group), and 本田技研工業 (Honda) — established a joint venture called 日本AIファウンデーションモデル開発 (Japan AI Foundation Model Development). Each founding member holds a stake exceeding 10%. Industrial heavyweights Nippon Steel (日本製鉄) and Kobe Steel (神戸製鋼所), as well as the three megabanks — MUFG, SMBC, and Mizuho — have also taken equity positions, giving the venture a cross-sectoral ownership structure that spans manufacturing, finance, and consumer electronics.

The strategic logic is focused on 物理AI (butsuri AI, "physical AI"): foundation models designed not for text generation but for real-world control tasks — perceiving environments through sensors and issuing commands to robot arms, factory actuators, and autonomous vehicles. The model targets roughly one trillion parameters and will be trained exclusively on Japanese operational data, processed on domestic infrastructure without routing through foreign cloud platforms. SoftBank and NEC will lead model development; Honda will be the first external deployer, integrating the model into its autonomous vehicle stack; Sony's involvement covers robotics and gaming hardware applications. プリファードネットワークス (Preferred Networks, PFN), the deep learning specialist with established ties to Toyota, is participating in the R&D phase.

Japan's 新エネルギー・産業技術総合開発機構 (NEDO, New Energy and Industrial Technology Development Organization) has earmarked approximately ¥1 trillion ($6.3B) in AI support over five years beginning in FY2026, and the new firm is widely expected to be the primary recipient. The 経済産業省 (METI) framed this as a sovereign infrastructure move, targeting a 30% share of the global physical AI market by 2040 — a claim backed by Japan's existing position: five of the world's ten largest industrial robot manufacturers are Japanese, giving the consortium industrial deployment channels few other national AI efforts can match. The tension worth noting for investors is that SoftBank simultaneously leads a $34.6B position in OpenAI — the paradigm the new venture is designed to route around.

STORY TWO
METI Approves Additional ¥631.5B for Rapidus — 2nm Pilot Line Active in Hokkaido, Mass Production Target Holds at FY2027
Reuters · Tech Funding News · The Register · April 11–14, 2026

Japan's 経済産業省 (METI) approved an additional ¥631.5 billion (~$4 billion) in funding for state-backed chipmaker ラピダス (Rapidus), bringing total government R&D support to approximately ¥2.35 trillion since the company's founding in August 2022. The fresh tranche is directed primarily at IIM-1, Rapidus's pilot facility in 千歳市 (Chitose City), Hokkaido, where engineers are running test wafers through a two-nanometer gate-all-around (ゲートオールアラウンド, GAA) process developed with IBM. METI also announced that its research affiliate NEDO will support semiconductor design projects alongside Fujitsu (富士通) and IBM Japan, with the explicit goal of building domestic demand for Rapidus's future products before mass production begins.

The operational milestone is more significant than the capital figure. As of mid-2025, IIM-1 completed its cleanroom buildout and installed extreme ultraviolet (極端紫外線, EUV) lithography tooling — the same equipment class TSMC and Samsung required to reach leading-edge nodes. Working two-nanometer prototypes are being produced, and CEO Atsuyoshi Koike (小池淳義) has maintained the FY2027 mass-production timeline publicly. TSMC and Samsung both entered 2nm volume production in 2025, meaning Rapidus will launch roughly two years behind the frontier; but Rapidus is not targeting the same customer base. Its model is a high-mix, low-volume, fast-turnaround foundry service aimed at customers seeking alternatives to the concentration risk of Taiwan — a proposition that matters to a specific and growing segment of the market. Early confirmed clients include Fujitsu and Canadian AI chip designer Tenstorrent.

The equity structure following February 2026's capital round now positions the Japanese government as Rapidus's largest single shareholder, holding 11.5% of voting rights plus a golden-share veto on strategic decisions. Private co-investors in that round included Canon (キヤノン), Fujitsu, and Sony. For institutional investors, the read is straightforward: Rapidus is no longer a venture bet but a strategic infrastructure program, funded with the same logic as a national power grid. The customer development challenge — finding sufficient orders to improve yields and justify operating costs ahead of 2027 — remains the pivotal near-term variable. Government subsidy covers capex; it cannot manufacture customers.

STORY THREE
BOJ Holds at 0.75% on Split Vote, Lifts CPI Forecast to 2.8% — OECD Projects Terminal Rate of 2% by End-2027
CNBC · Bloomberg · Japan Times · April 28 & May 13, 2026

The 日本銀行 (Nippon Ginkō, Bank of Japan / BOJ) left its 政策金利 (seisaku kinri, policy rate) unchanged at 0.75% at its April 27–28 meeting, but the decision was notably fractious: three of the nine board members dissented, proposing an immediate increase to 1.0%. The hawks argued that Middle East tensions — specifically, the Iran conflict's pressure on oil prices — have skewed inflation risks to the upside. The BOJ's core CPI forecast was raised to 2.8% from its prior 1.9% projection, a substantial revision that reflects both imported energy cost pressure and underlying wage-price dynamics that BOJ economists have been monitoring since the shuntō (春闘) spring wage negotiations produced average raises exceeding 5% for the second consecutive year.

BOJ Deputy Governor Ryozo Himino (氷見野良三) signaled in post-meeting commentary that the board intends to "continue to raise the policy rate and adjust the degree of 金融緩和 (kin'yū kanwa, monetary accommodation) in accordance with economic, price, and financial developments," while cautioning that geopolitical uncertainty complicates the timing of further moves. The OECD, in its May 13 economic outlook, published a projection that the BOJ's benchmark rate will reach 2% by the end of 2027 — a path that would imply roughly five additional 25-basis-point increments from the current 0.75% level, spread over approximately six to seven policy meetings. That trajectory, if realized, would make Japanese government bonds (JGBs) materially more competitive with global fixed income peers and would likely put further pressure on the yen's carry-trade funding role.

For equity investors with Japan exposure, the rate path has asymmetric read-throughs. Banks — notably 三菱UFJフィナンシャル・グループ (MUFG), 三井住友フィナンシャルグループ (SMFG), and みずほフィナンシャルグループ (Mizuho) — benefit from net interest margin expansion at each increment. Export-oriented manufacturers and the broader Nikkei, by contrast, face headwinds from yen appreciation pressure and higher domestic borrowing costs. The BOJ's next scheduled meeting is June 16–17; with the April dissent pattern on record, markets are pricing roughly a 35% probability of a June hike to 1.0%, contingent on whether May CPI data, due June 20, confirms the upward trend.
